# Impact of climate-induced human migration on dengue exposure risk in Africa

Authors: Moges DM., Poongavanan J., Dor G.,, Moir M., Baxter C., Oliveira T., Moritz MUG., Kraemer M., Tegally H.

# Abstract

Climate-induced human migration could become an important factor shaping infectious disease transmission across Africa. Using continent-wide projections of climate-related migration and population change under multiple development and emissions scenarios through 2050, we examine the impacts of population exposure to dengue virus due to climate migration. Here we examine how internal and cross-border migration shift across space and time, and how these shifts reshape population exposure to dengue. By mid‑century, climate change could force about 1.1 million people to move across borders and nearly 75 million to relocate internally. Internal migration is expected to concentrate people in cities, border areas, and climatically favorable transmission regions. Together, these mobility patterns create conditions for dengue to spread into previously unaffected parts of Southern Africa and intensify its burden in endemic regions of West and East Africa. Overall, climate‑driven migration in Africa will likely increase future dengue risk, underscoring the need for early public‑health planning that considers migration as a core contributor.
